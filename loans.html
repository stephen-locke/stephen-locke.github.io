FHA Loan — Federal Housing Administration

Backed by HUD. Low down payment and flexible credit requirements make this the most common first-time buyer program. Appraisals follow HUD Handbook 4000.1 and must confirm both market value and compliance with Minimum Property Standards (MPS).

🔑 Core Appraisal Principles
  • Dual purpose: establishes market value AND confirms HUD Minimum Property Standards
  • Property must be Safe — protects occupant health and safety
  • Property must be Sound — structurally sound and in good repair
  • Property must be Secure — protects the lender's collateral
  • Must be appraised by an FHA-approved appraiser
🏗️ Roof & Structure
  • Roof must have 2+ years of remaining life (appraiser estimates)
  • No missing or damaged shingles allowing moisture intrusion
  • Foundation must be structurally sound with no major cracks
  • Crawl spaces must be accessible and adequately ventilated
⚠️ Safety Hazards
  • No exposed or frayed wiring or unsafe electrical panels
  • Working smoke detectors on every level
  • No chipping or peeling paint (lead paint risk — especially pre-1978 homes)
  • Handrails required on all stairs with 3 or more steps
  • No trip hazards or broken flooring
💧 Water & Plumbing
  • Hot and cold running water in all bathrooms and kitchen
  • Water heater must meet local codes and be properly strapped in seismic areas
  • No evidence of active leaks
  • Private wells require water quality testing
  • Septic systems must be functional; inspection often required
🌡️ Heating & Mechanical
  • Adequate heat in all rooms — minimum 50°F in living areas
  • If central A/C exists, it must be functional
  • No unvented fuel-burning heaters as primary heat source
🚪 Interior Conditions
  • No dampness, water damage, or evidence of mold
  • All rooms must have adequate egress (windows/doors for emergency exit)
  • Bedrooms must have windows that open
  • No major pest or termite damage; WDO inspection may be required
🏡 Exterior & Site
  • No standing water pooling near the foundation
  • Driveway/access must be passable year-round
  • No encroachments or easement issues that affect use
  • Well and septic must be adequate distance apart — typically 50–100 ft

FHA Appraisal vs. Home Inspection An FHA appraisal is NOT a substitute for a home inspection. It catches major deficiencies but does not evaluate HVAC systems, plumbing depth, or electrical capacity in detail. A separate home inspection is strongly recommended for the buyer's protection.

VA Loan — Department of Veterans Affairs

Available to active duty, veterans, and surviving spouses. Zero down, no PMI, generally the best terms available. Appraisals follow VA Pamphlet 26-7 and must be conducted by a VA-certified appraiser confirming Minimum Property Requirements (MPRs).

PMI Required
No
Funding Fee
Varies
Appraisal Standard
VA Pam. 26-7
Termite Inspection
Most States
🔑 Core MPR Principles
  • Must be appraised by a VA-certified appraiser only
  • Property must be Safe — protects health and safety of occupants
  • Property must be Structurally Sound — no deferred maintenance threatening integrity
  • Property must be Sanitary — adequate sanitation and water/sewage systems
🏗️ Roof & Structure
  • Roof must be in good condition with reasonable future utility
  • Maximum 2 layers of roofing — must replace if exceeded
  • Flat roofs subject to additional scrutiny
  • No significant settlement, excessive cracks, or deterioration in foundation
  • Crawl spaces must have adequate ventilation and be free of standing water
  • Basement must be dry and properly drained
📐 Space & Access
  • Sufficient space for living, sleeping, cooking, and sanitation
  • Continuous, all-weather road access required
  • Access to each unit must not pass through another unit (multi-unit)
🌡️ Mechanical Systems
  • Heating must be adequate for the climate and safe
  • Wood-burning stoves alone are not acceptable as sole heat source
  • Plumbing must be functional with hot and cold water
  • Electrical must have adequate capacity with no exposed wiring
💧 Water & Sewage
  • Private wells must meet local authority standards; water test required
  • Septic systems must be functional and not posing health risk
  • Distance requirements between well and septic must meet local codes
  • No cross-connections between potable and non-potable water
🌍 Site & Location Hazards
  • No high-voltage power lines or transmission towers nearby
  • No oil or gas wells on the property
  • No sinkholes or unstable soil
  • Flood zone properties require flood insurance (not an automatic disqualifier)
  • Proximity to airport noise zones must be disclosed
  • Excessive noise from highways, airports, or industrial sites is evaluated
🪲 Termite & Pest
  • Termite inspection (WDIR) required in most states
  • VA designates which states require mandatory inspections
  • Active infestation or structural damage must be treated and repaired before closing

A unique VA process triggered when the appraiser believes the sale price may exceed market value:

  • Appraiser issues a Tidewater notice before completing the appraisal
  • Lender/agent has 48 hours to submit comparable sales data
  • Appraiser considers the data before finalizing value
  • If value still comes in low, buyer/seller must renegotiate — VA prohibits financing above appraised value

If the appraisal comes in low, the buyer or lender can formally request a Reconsideration of Value:

  • Must provide new or better comparable sales to support a higher value
  • Original appraiser reviews and may revise the value
  • A second appraisal can be requested but is rarely granted
VA vs. FHA: Key Distinction VA appraisals tend to be stricter than FHA in certain areas — termite requirements, road access, noise/hazard evaluation, and roof layering limits — reflecting the VA's commitment to protecting veteran buyers from substandard properties.

USDA Loan — U.S. Department of Agriculture

Rural Development program with zero down payment for eligible rural areas. Follows HUD Handbook 4000.1 (same as FHA), but adds its own eligibility and modesty overlays. Location and property type eligibility are as important as condition.

📍 Property Eligibility (USDA Unique)
  • Must be in a USDA-designated rural area — generally towns under 35,000 population
  • Eligibility verified at usda.gov by address — not determined by appraiser
  • Many suburban areas near major cities are ineligible even if they feel rural
  • Must be the borrower's primary residence
  • No investment properties or vacation homes

✨ Modesty Standard (USDA Unique)
  • No specific square footage cap, but home must be typical for the area
  • Properties with in-ground pools are allowable but flagged for modesty review
  • No accessory dwelling units intended for income production
  • Site size must be typical for the area — large acreage can trigger additional review
🏗️ Minimum Property Standards
  • Roof must be in good condition with reasonable remaining life; no active leaks
  • Foundation structurally sound, no major cracks or settlement
  • Adequate heating for climate; functional plumbing with hot and cold water
  • Electrical system must be safe with adequate capacity
  • Crawl spaces must be dry, ventilated, and accessible
  • Pre-1978 homes require chipping/peeling paint to be remediated
💧 Water & Wastewater
  • Private wells — water quality test required; must meet EPA/state standards
  • Septic systems — must be functional; inspection typically required
  • Well-to-septic separation must meet local health codes
  • Community water/sewer preferred where available
  • Tribal/community-operated systems must be functional and reliable
🌿 Site Conditions
  • No drainage issues or standing water near foundation
  • No environmental hazards (underground storage tanks, contamination)
  • Must have adequate all-weather road access
  • Flood zone properties require flood insurance — not an automatic disqualifier
  • WDO (termite) inspection required in high-risk states

🏗️ New Construction Requirements
  • Must meet local building codes
  • Requires a one-year builder warranty
  • Final inspection required before loan closes
  • Must have thermal standards (insulation requirements) per HUD guidelines
Key USDA Distinction USDA appraisals are unique in that location and modesty are as important as condition. A perfectly maintained home in a non-rural area or with luxury features can still fail USDA eligibility — something not a factor in FHA or VA appraisals.

HUD Section 184 — Native American Home Loan

Designed for Native American and Alaska Native tribal members. Follows HUD 4000.1 for property condition, but the most distinctive aspects involve trust land eligibility, leasehold requirements, and BIA approvals. Appraiser experience with tribal land is critical.

📋 Leasehold Requirements
  • Lease term must extend at least 25 years beyond the loan maturity date
  • Lease must be HUD-approved and allow for foreclosure/assignment if needed
  • Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) approval often required
  • Tribal authorization or consent may be required depending on the tribe
📊 Comparable Sales Challenges
  • Finding comparable sales on or near tribal land is often difficult
  • Appraisers may use comparables from off-reservation areas with adjustments
  • HUD allows broader geographic search for comps than conventional appraisals
  • Some reservations have very thin or no sales history — appraiser must justify methodology
🏗️ Minimum Property Standards
  • Roof must be in good condition with reasonable remaining useful life
  • Foundation structurally sound; crawl spaces dry, accessible, and ventilated
  • Adequate heating for climate — especially critical in tribal areas with harsh winters
  • Functional plumbing with hot and cold running water
  • Electrical system must be safe with no exposed wiring
  • Pre-1978 homes require lead paint identification and remediation
💧 Water & Wastewater
  • Private wells — water quality test required; must meet safe drinking water standards
  • Septic systems — must be functional; inspection typically required
  • Tribal community water/sewer systems must be functional and reliable
  • Well-to-septic separation must meet local or tribal health codes
⚠️ Practical Challenges Unique to Section 184
  • Appraiser availability — few appraisers are experienced with trust land; can delay process
  • Thin comparable market — limited sales data on many reservations
  • BIA processing times — trust land approvals can add weeks or months to closing
  • Tribal approval requirements — some tribes require right of first refusal or consent
  • Jurisdictional complexity — state laws may not fully apply on tribal land

Key Section 184 Distinction Section 184 appraisals are technically similar to FHA in property condition standards, but the land tenure and title complexity is what sets them apart. An appraiser without trust land experience can significantly undervalue the property or miss approval requirements — making appraiser selection especially critical.

Conventional Loans — Secondary Market & Portfolio

Conventional loans have far fewer mandated property condition requirements than government-backed loans. Standards shift depending on whether the loan is sold to Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac (secondary market) or held in-house by the lender as a portfolio loan.

What Fannie/Freddie Do NOT Require (unlike government loans):

  • Specific health and safety repairs (unless they affect value/marketability)
  • Termite inspections (unless required by state law or flagged by appraiser)
  • Well/water testing
  • Lead paint remediation
  • Handrails, smoke detectors, or other safety items

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac use a standardized Condition Rating scale:

OptionDescription
Traditional AppraisalFull interior/exterior physical inspection

Waivers and desktop options are determined by Fannie's DU or Freddie's LPA automated underwriting systems. Typically available for lower LTV, strong credit, and well-documented properties.

Portfolio loans are held on the lender's own books and never sold to Fannie/Freddie, giving lenders maximum flexibility. Standards are governed by:

  • USPAP — Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice; federally required for all appraisals tied to federally regulated lenders
  • FIRREA — Appraisals required for transactions over $500,000; below that, an evaluation may suffice
  • OCC, FDIC, or Federal Reserve bank examiner guidelines depending on charter type

Portfolio Lender Flexibility:

  • May accept in-house appraisers (not required to use AMC)
  • May use AVMs (Automated Valuation Models) for lower-risk transactions
  • Can set their own condition thresholds — more or less flexible than Fannie/Freddie
  • May require additional inspections at their discretion (roof, foundation, environmental)
  • Non-warrantable condos, mixed-use, large acreage, or unique properties often go portfolio

Key Conventional Takeaway A property that fails FHA/VA/USDA standards can often still be financed conventionally if the condition doesn't impair market value. The appraiser notes conditions but is not required to flag them as repairs unless they affect value — a significant difference from government loans.
